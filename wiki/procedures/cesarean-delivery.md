## Clinical Definition

Cesarean delivery (cesarean section) is the delivery of the fetus through incisions in the abdominal wall and uterus. It is one of the most common surgical procedures worldwide and is indicated when vaginal delivery would risk maternal or fetal health (1A).

## Indications

- **Maternal:** Prior uterine surgery (especially classical incision), obstructed labor, placenta previa, placental abruption with fetal distress, active genital herpes lesions
- **Fetal:** Nonreassuring fetal heart rate, breech presentation (term singleton), transverse lie, cord prolapse
- **Combined:** Failed progress, failed operative vaginal delivery, elective repeat cesarean

## Technique Key Points

- **Skin incision:** Pfannenstiel (transverse) most common; Maylard or Cherney for extended exposure; vertical midline for emergency speed
- **Uterine incision:** Low transverse (Kerr) preferred; low vertical (Kronig) or classical (upper segment) for preterm, malpresentation, or anterior placenta previa
- **Active management third stage:** Oxytocin prophylaxis to reduce blood loss (1A)

## Evidence for Efficacy

- Lifesaving in true obstetric emergencies (1A)
- Elective cesarean at 39 weeks reduces perinatal mortality vs 38 weeks (1B)
- Overuse in low-risk settings associated with increased maternal morbidity without neonatal benefit (1A)

## Contraindications and Precautions

- No absolute contraindications when maternal/fetal life threatened
- TOLAC (trial of labor after cesarean): uterine rupture risk ~0.5–1% with low transverse, higher with classical or T-incision
- Placenta accreta spectrum: major hemorrhage risk; planned delivery at tertiary center with multidisciplinary team

## Complications

- Hemorrhage (most common)
- Infection (endometritis, wound infection)
- Thromboembolic events
- Injury to bladder, bowel, ureter
- Placenta accreta spectrum in subsequent pregnancies (risk increases with each cesarean)
- Neonatal: transient tachypnea, iatrogenic prematurity if elective at <39 weeks
