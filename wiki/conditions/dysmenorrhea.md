# Dysmenorrhea

**Dysmenorrhea** is cyclic pelvic pain associated with menstruation. Primary dysmenorrhea results from prostaglandin-mediated uterine contractions without pelvic pathology; secondary dysmenorrhea results from identifiable disease such as endometriosis, adenomyosis, leiomyomas, pelvic infection, or obstructive Müllerian anomalies.

## Scope

This page addresses evaluation and management of dysmenorrhea in reproductive-aged patients, including when to treat empirically, when to investigate for secondary causes, and how to identify endometriosis or other pelvic pathology.

## Epidemiology

- Dysmenorrhea is among the most common gynecologic symptoms in adolescents and reproductive-aged adults.
- Symptoms may impair school, work, sleep, and quality of life.
- Primary dysmenorrhea typically begins within 6-12 months after menarche, after ovulatory cycles are established.
- Secondary dysmenorrhea is more likely with progressive pain, non-cyclic pelvic pain, dyspareunia, infertility, abnormal bleeding, or poor response to first-line therapy.

## Pathophysiology

### Primary Dysmenorrhea

Withdrawal of progesterone before menses triggers endometrial prostaglandin production, especially prostaglandin F2α. This causes uterine hypercontractility, vasoconstriction, ischemia, and cramping pain. Associated systemic symptoms include nausea, diarrhea, fatigue, headache, and dizziness.

### Secondary Dysmenorrhea

Common etiologies:

| Etiology | Clues |
|---|---|
| **Endometriosis** | Progressive pain, deep dyspareunia, dyschezia, infertility, family history |
| **Adenomyosis** | Heavy menstrual bleeding, enlarged tender uterus, age >35 but can occur earlier |
| **Leiomyomas** | Heavy bleeding, bulk symptoms, enlarged irregular uterus |
| **Pelvic inflammatory disease** | Cervical motion tenderness, discharge, fever, STI risk |
| **Obstructive anomaly** | Severe pain soon after menarche, primary amenorrhea, hematocolpos |

## Diagnosis

### Initial Assessment

1. **Pregnancy test** if sexually active or pregnancy possible.
2. Pain history:
   - Timing relative to menses
   - Age at onset
   - Progression over time
   - Response to NSAIDs/hormonal therapy
   - Dyspareunia, dyschezia, urinary symptoms
3. Bleeding pattern and anemia symptoms.
4. Sexual history and STI risk.
5. Impact on activities and analgesic use.

### Physical Examination

- Pelvic exam is not required before empiric treatment in adolescents with classic primary dysmenorrhea and no red flags.
- Pelvic exam is indicated for sexually active patients with infection symptoms, severe pain, adnexal mass concern, abnormal bleeding, or suspected secondary dysmenorrhea.

### Imaging

**Transvaginal ultrasound** is first-line imaging when secondary dysmenorrhea is suspected. It evaluates ovarian endometriomas, leiomyomas, adenomyosis features, adnexal masses, and obstructive anatomy.

MRI is reserved for complex Müllerian anomalies, deep infiltrating endometriosis mapping, or uncertain ultrasound findings.

## Red Flags Requiring Further Evaluation

- Pain beginning immediately with menarche or primary amenorrhea
- Progressive worsening or new severe dysmenorrhea
- Non-cyclic pelvic pain
- Abnormal uterine bleeding
- Dyspareunia, dyschezia, or infertility
- Pelvic mass
- Fever, purulent discharge, cervical motion tenderness
- Failure of empiric therapy after 3-6 months

## Management

### First-Line: NSAIDs

NSAIDs inhibit cyclooxygenase-mediated prostaglandin synthesis and are first-line for primary dysmenorrhea.

Practical regimen:

1. Start 1-2 days before expected menses when cycles are predictable.
2. Continue scheduled dosing for 48-72 hours after bleeding begins.
3. Take with food; avoid in renal disease, active peptic ulcer disease, anticoagulation risk, or NSAID hypersensitivity.

Common options include ibuprofen, naproxen, and mefenamic acid. Choice is usually guided by tolerability, dosing schedule, and contraindications.

### First-Line Alternative or Add-On: Hormonal Suppression

Hormonal therapy reduces ovulation, endometrial proliferation, and menstrual prostaglandin burden.

Options:

| Method | Use Case |
|---|---|
| Combined oral contraceptive | Cyclic or continuous suppression; useful with acne/contraception goals |
| Vaginal ring / patch | Non-daily combined hormonal options |
| Progestin-only pill | Estrogen contraindication |
| DMPA | Strong suppression; consider bone density/bleeding counseling |
| Etonogestrel implant | Contraception plus suppression; irregular bleeding common |
| LNG-IUD | Useful if heavy bleeding or suspected adenomyosis/endometriosis |

Continuous regimens are often preferred when pain is severe or endometriosis is suspected.

### Suspected Endometriosis

If symptoms persist despite 3-6 months of NSAIDs plus hormonal suppression, evaluate for endometriosis and other secondary causes. Options include:

- Pelvic ultrasound to assess endometrioma or other pathology
- Empiric continuous hormonal suppression if imaging is normal
- Referral for laparoscopy when pain is refractory, diagnosis uncertain, fertility is desired, or deep disease is suspected

### Non-Pharmacologic Adjuncts

- Heat therapy to lower abdomen
- Exercise as tolerated
- Sleep optimization and school/work accommodations during severe episodes
- TENS, acupuncture, or behavioral pain strategies may help selected patients but should not delay evaluation of red flags
