## Clinical Definition

Fetal anomalies (congenital anomalies, birth defects) are structural or functional abnormalities present at conception or developing during intrauterine life (2B). Major anomalies are those causing significant medical, surgical, or cosmetic consequences. Minor anomalies are structural deviations without serious medical or cosmetic significance.

## Epidemiology

- Overall incidence: 2–3% of all live births have a major structural anomaly (2B)
- An additional 5–10% have minor anomalies (2B)
- Approximately 50% of major anomalies are detected by second-trimester ultrasound (2B)
- Leading causes of infant mortality in developed countries (2B)
- Most common systems affected: cardiovascular (20%), CNS/neural tube (10%), genitourinary (10%), gastrointestinal (5%)

## Risk Factors

### Maternal (2B)
- Advanced maternal age (>35): increased chromosomal aneuploidy risk
- Diabetes mellitus (pre-gestational): cardiac, neural tube, caudal regression
- Obesity: increased NTD and cardiac defects
- Folic acid deficiency: neural tube defects (NTDs)
- Teratogen exposure: alcohol (fetal alcohol syndrome), antiepileptics (valproate, carbamazepine), isotretinoin, warfarin, ACE inhibitors
- Infections: rubella, CMV, toxoplasmosis, Zika, varicella (first trimester)

### Fetal/Placental (2B)
- Polyhydramnios (often associated with GI obstruction, anencephaly)
- Oligohydramnios (often associated with renal agenesis, obstructive uropathy)
- Multiple gestation: increased structural anomaly rate per fetus
- Fetal growth restriction: associated with chromosomal and syndromic anomalies

## Classification

### By Organ System (2B)
| System | Common Anomalies |
|--------|-----------------|
| CNS | Anencephaly, spina bifida, hydrocephalus, holoprosencephaly |
| Cardiac | VSD, ASD, tetralogy of Fallot, hypoplastic left heart, transposition |
| Genitourinary | Renal agenesis, polycystic kidney, hydronephrosis, bladder outlet obstruction |
| GI | Esophageal atresia, duodenal atresia, gastroschisis, omphalocele, anorectal malformation |
| Craniofacial | Cleft lip/palate, micrognathia |
| Skeletal | Limb reduction, skeletal dysplasias, clubfoot |
| Thoracic | CDH, CPAM (CCAM), pleural effusion |

### By Etiology
- **Chromosomal:** Trisomy 21, 18, 13; Turner syndrome; triploidy
- **Single gene/syndromic:** Autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease, Meckel-Gruber
- **Multifactorial:** Most cardiac defects, NTDs, orofacial clefts
- **Teratogenic:** Fetal alcohol syndrome, valproate embryopathy
- **Idiopathic:** ~50% of major anomalies

## Screening and Diagnosis

### First Trimester (2B)
- **Nuchal translucency (NT) + serum screening (PAPP-A, free β-hCG):** 85–90% detection for trisomies 21 and 18
- **Cell-free DNA (cfDNA/NIPT):** >99% sensitivity for trisomies 21, 18, 13; limited for sex chromosome aneuploidies and microdeletions
- **Early anatomy scan:** 11–14 weeks can detect major anomalies (acrania, omphalocele, limb defects)

### Second Trimester (2B)
- **Detailed anatomy survey:** 18–22 weeks; detection rate ~50–70% for major anomalies depending on operator and equipment
- **Soft markers:** echogenic intracardiac focus, choroid plexus cyst, pyelectasis, short long bones; generally low predictive value in low-risk patients
- **Amniocentesis / CVS:** Karyotype, microarray, or targeted genetic testing when anomalies or risk factors identified

### Third Trimester (2B)
- Growth ultrasound may detect late-appearing anomalies or progression
- Fetal echocardiography if cardiac anomaly suspected or risk factors present
- MRI increasingly used for CNS and thoracic anomalies (better tissue characterization than ultrasound)

## Associated Ultrasound Findings

- **Polyhydramnios:** GI obstruction (esophageal atresia, duodenal atresia), anencephaly, facial clefts (impaired swallowing)
- **Oligohydramnios:** Bilateral renal agenesis (Potter sequence), obstructive uropathy, severe IUGR
- **Fetal hydrops:** Immune (Rh alloimmunization) or non-immune (anemia, cardiac, genetic)

## Evidence-based Management

### Prenatal
- **Multidisciplinary counseling:** Maternal-fetal medicine, pediatric surgery, genetics, neonatology, social work
- **Fetal intervention:** Selective cases — open fetal surgery for spina bifida (MOMS trial), fetal cardiac interventions (aortic stenosis with evolving HLHS), laser for TTTS
- **Delivery planning:** Mode, timing, location (tertiary center with NICU and pediatric surgery)
- **Termination of pregnancy:** Legal availability varies; offered in severe/lethal anomalies

### Intrapartum
- **Cesarean delivery:** Not routinely indicated for anomalies unless obstetrically indicated or for EXIT procedure (ex utero intrapartum treatment for airway obstruction)
- **EXIT procedure:** Maintains uteroplacental circulation while securing airway for cervical teratoma, severe micrognathia, CPAM with mediastinal shift

### Postnatal
- Early pediatric surgical referral for GI obstruction, CDH, abdominal wall defects
- Cardiac surgery planning for critical congenital heart disease
- Early intervention services for neurodevelopmental outcomes

## Prognosis

Highly variable by anomaly type:
- **Lethal:** Anencephaly, bilateral renal agenesis, trisomy 13/18 (most cases)
- **Surgically correctable with good outcomes:** Gastroschisis, omphalocele, most VSD/ASD, pyloric stenosis
- **Complex chronic disease:** CDH, HLHS, spina bifida — survival improving but long-term morbidity significant
- **Isolated minor anomalies:** Excellent prognosis

## Complications

- Polyhydramnios → preterm labor, PPROM, cord prolapse
- Oligohydramnios → pulmonary hypoplasia, Potter sequence, fetal compression
- Fetal demise in utero
- Stillbirth
- Neonatal death from severe cardiac, pulmonary, or renal anomalies
- Long-term neurodevelopmental impairment (especially CNS and cardiac anomalies)

## Active Research / Open Questions

- AI-assisted anomaly detection from routine ultrasound
- In utero gene therapy and stem cell therapy trials
- Expanded cfDNA screening for microdeletions and single-gene disorders
- Long-term outcomes after fetal cardiac interventions
- Impact of maternal metabolic environment (obesity, diabetes) on anomaly risk

## Evidence Summary

| Claim | Evidence Level | Basis |
|-------|-------------|-------|
| Major anomalies occur in 2–3% of live births | 2B | Population birth defect registries |
| Second-trimester ultrasound detects ~50% major anomalies | 2B | Large prospective cohorts (Eurofetus, etc.) |
| cfDNA >99% sensitive for trisomy 21 | 1B | Multiple large prospective validation studies |
| Folic acid supplementation reduces NTD risk by ~70% | 1A | RCTs and meta-analyses |
| MOMS trial: fetal surgery improves spina bifida outcomes | 1B | Single RCT with follow-up data |
