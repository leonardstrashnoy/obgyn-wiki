## Clinical Definition

Preeclampsia is a pregnancy-specific multisystem disorder characterized by new-onset hypertension after 20 weeks gestation accompanied by proteinuria or other end-organ dysfunction (thrombocytopenia, impaired liver function, renal insufficiency, pulmonary edema, or cerebral/visual symptoms) (2B). It is a major cause of maternal and perinatal morbidity and mortality worldwide.

## Epidemiology

- **Incidence:** Affects approximately 2–8% of pregnancies globally (2B)
- **Risk factors** (2B):
  - Primiparity (first pregnancy)
  - Advanced maternal age (>35 years)
  - Obesity / elevated BMI
  - Pre-existing diabetes mellitus
  - Chronic hypertension
  - Multiple pregnancy (twins/triplets)
  - Prior history of preeclampsia
  - Family history / genetic susceptibility
  - Placental abruption (strong association)

## Pathophysiology

The disease follows a **two-stage model** (2B):

1. **Stage 1 — Placental phase:** Inadequate trophoblast invasion and incomplete spiral artery remodeling lead to placental hypoperfusion, hypoxia, and oxidative stress.
2. **Stage 2 — Maternal syndrome:** The hypoxic placenta releases anti-angiogenic and pro-inflammatory factors including:
   - Soluble fms-like tyrosine kinase-1 (**sFlt-1**)
   - Soluble endoglin (**sEng**)
   
   These trigger systemic **endothelial dysfunction**, hypertension, and multi-organ involvement.

Additional mechanisms (2B):
- **Angiogenic imbalance:** Excess anti-angiogenic factors vs. pro-angiogenic factors
- **Immune dysregulation:** Th1/Th2/Th17/Treg imbalance; uterine NK cell dysfunction at maternal-fetal interface
- **microRNA-mediated epigenetic regulation:** Placental hypoxia alters miRNA expression affecting trophoblast invasion and angiogenesis

## Diagnostic Criteria

- **Blood pressure:** ≥140/90 mmHg on two occasions ≥4 hours apart after 20 weeks gestation in previously normotensive woman (2B)
- **Proteinuria:** ≥300 mg/24h or protein/creatinine ratio ≥0.3 (2B)
- **Severe features** (any of the following) (2B):
  - BP ≥160/110 mmHg
  - Thrombocytopenia (platelets <100,000/μL)
  - Impaired liver function (transaminases ≥2× normal)
  - Renal insufficiency (creatinine >1.1 mg/dL or doubling)
  - Pulmonary edema
  - New-onset cerebral/visual symptoms

## Evidence-based Management

### Prevention
- **Low-dose aspirin** for high-risk nulliparous women or those with prior preeclampsia (1A)
- Blood pressure optimization before conception

### Antepartum Management
1. **Blood pressure control:** Labetalol, nifedipine, or methyldopa (antihypertensives safe in pregnancy) (1B)
2. **Fetal surveillance:** Weekly NST/BPP after diagnosis; serial growth ultrasounds (2B)
3. **Laboratory monitoring:** CBC, liver enzymes, creatinine every 1–2 weeks (2B)
4. **Magnesium sulfate** prophylaxis for severe preeclampsia to prevent eclampsia (1A)

### Delivery Timing
- **Severe preeclampsia:** Delivery at 34 weeks or upon diagnosis if maternal/fetal instability (1A)
- **Mild/without severe features:** Expectant management to 37 weeks with close monitoring (1B)
- **Eclampsia:** Immediate delivery after maternal stabilization (1A)

### Postpartum
- Continue BP monitoring 72 hours postpartum and at 7–10 days (2B)
- Risk of AKI persists postpartum; monitor renal function (2B)

## Complications

| Complication | Evidence |
|-------------|----------|
| **Eclampsia** (seizures) | Preventable with magnesium sulfate (1A) |
| **HELLP syndrome** | Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets — obstetric emergency (2B) |
| **Acute kidney injury** | Rates increasing; 160.1 per 10,000 deliveries among preeclamptic patients (2B) |
| **Placental abruption** | Strong association; shared risk factors and pathophysiology (2B) |
| **Pulmonary edema** | Stable rates, weak inverse association with AKI trends (2B) |
| **Stroke / DIC** | Rare but life-threatening (2B) |
| **IUGR / Preterm birth** | Common fetal complications (2B) |

## Controversies / Contested Evidence

- **AKI trend paradox:** Despite stable pulmonary edema rates, AKI among preeclamptic patients has increased significantly (rate ratio 1.46, 95% CI 1.07–1.99). Adjustment for risk factors does **not** explain this temporal increase — suggesting changing diagnostic practices or unmeasured confounders (2B).
- **Implementation gap:** Clinical practice guidelines exist for preeclampsia prevention and management, but implementation remains poor in low-resource settings (2A).

## Bottom Line

Preeclampsia is a placental disorder with systemic endothelial consequences. Early identification of risk factors, low-dose aspirin prophylaxis for high-risk women, and magnesium sulfate for severe disease are the cornerstones of management. Delivery remains the only definitive cure — timing balances maternal safety against fetal maturity.

This frequency shift of the returning signal is displayed in a graphic form as a time-dependent plot. In this display, the vertical axis represents the frequency shift, and the horizontal axis represents the temporal change of this frequency shift as it relates to the events of the cardiac cycle (Fig. 22-1). This frequency shift is highest during systole, when the blood flow is at its fastest, and lowest during end diastole, when the blood flow is at its slowest in the peripheral circula- tion. Given that the velocity of flow in a particular vascular bed is inversely proportional to the downstream impedance to flow, the fre- quency shift therefore derives information on the downstream imped- ance to flow of the vascular bed under study. The frequency shift is also dependent on the cosine of the angle that the ultrasound beam makes with the direction of blood flow in the targeted blood vessel (see formula in Fig. 22-1). When the sound beam is parallel to the blood vessel of interest and thus the moving red blood cells, the entire Doppler shift is measured. However, when an angle exists between the direction of flow and the sound beam, the measured velocity is less than the true velocity.2 The degree to which the velocity is accurately measured depends on the angle of incidence (cosine Q). At an angle of 0 degrees, the measured velocity is equal to the true velocity. When the sound beam is perpendicular to the direction of flow (90 degrees), the measured velocity is zero, because the cosine of 90 degrees is zero.2 Thus, ideally, one wants to measure the velocity with as small an angle as possible. Given that the insonating angle is difficult to measure in clinical practice, indices that rely on ratios of frequency shifts were developed to quantitate Doppler waveforms. By relying on ratios of frequency shifts, these Doppler indices are thus independent of the effects of the insonating angle of the ultrasound beam.
