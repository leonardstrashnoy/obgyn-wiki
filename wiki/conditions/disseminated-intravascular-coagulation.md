## Clinical Definition

Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) is an acquired clinicopathologic syndrome characterized by widespread activation of coagulation, fibrin deposition in microvasculature, consumption of clotting factors and platelets, and secondary fibrinolysis leading to bleeding (2B). In obstetrics, it is most often triggered by abruption with concealed hemorrhage, amniotic fluid embolism, sepsis, or retained dead fetus.

## Diagnosis

- **Labs:** Prolonged PT/INR, prolonged aPTT, thrombocytopenia, elevated D-dimer, low fibrinogen
- **Scoring systems:** ISTH DIC score (platelets, fibrinogen, PT, D-dimer)
- **Clinical:** Oozing from IV sites, petechiae, ecchymoses, uncontrolled bleeding

## Obstetric Triggers

- Placental abruption (especially concealed / Grade 3)
- Amniotic fluid embolism
- Severe sepsis / chorioamnionitis
- Retained IUFD
- Severe preeclampsia / HELLP
- Massive hemorrhage / transfusion

## Management

- **Treat the underlying cause:** Delivery for abruption; antibiotics for sepsis; evacuation for IUFD
- **Blood product replacement:**
  - FFP for prolonged PT/aPTT
  - Cryoprecipitate / fibrinogen concentrate for fibrinogen < 100–150 mg/dL
  - Platelets if < 10–20,000 or active bleeding
- **Tranexamic acid:** 1 g IV if fibrinolysis dominant (2B)
- **Heparin:** Rarely indicated in obstetric DIC (mainly for chronic low-grade DIC or AFE later stage); generally contraindicated in acute bleeding
