## Clinical Definition / what it is

Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) is excessive bleeding following childbirth (delivery of the placenta) (2B). It is a significant cause of maternal morbidity and mortality worldwide. While definitions vary slightly, PPH is commonly defined as blood loss greater than 500 mL after vaginal delivery or greater than 1000 mL after cesarean delivery (2B). However, any postpartum bleeding that causes hemodynamic instability or requires transfusion should be considered PPH, regardless of the volume (2B).

## Epidemiology (incidence, risk factors if known)

PPH is a common obstetric complication, occurring in approximately 1-5% of deliveries (2B). Risk factors include:
*   Uterine atony (the most common cause)
*   Retained placental tissue
*   Lacerations of the genital tract
*   Coagulation disorders
*   Previous history of PPH
*   Multiple gestation
*   Macrosomia (large baby)
*   Prolonged labor
*   Induction or augmentation of labor
*   Certain medical conditions like [[preeclampsia]]

## Pathophysiology summary

The most common cause of PPH is uterine atony, which is the failure of the uterus to contract adequately after delivery (2B). Normally, uterine contractions compress the blood vessels supplying the placental site, preventing excessive bleeding. When the uterus fails to contract (atony), these vessels remain open, leading to significant hemorrhage (2B). Other causes include trauma to the birth canal (lacerations), retained products of conception (placenta or membranes), and coagulation abnormalities (2B). Sheehan's syndrome, a rare cause of hypopituitarism, can result from ischemic necrosis of the pituitary gland following severe PPH (2B).

## Evidence-based Management (with evidence levels cited inline)

Management of PPH involves prompt recognition and intervention, often following a stepwise approach:

1.  **Uterine Massage:** Fundal massage helps stimulate uterine contraction (2B).
2.  **Uterotonics:** Medications to contract the uterus are first-line treatment.
    *   [[Oxytocin]] is the preferred agent (2B). Prophylactic oxytocin infusion can reduce blood loss (2B).
    *   Other options include [[misoprostol]], [[methylergonovine]], and [[carboprost]] (2B).
3.  **Fluid Resuscitation:** Intravenous fluids are crucial to maintain hemodynamic stability (2B). Blood transfusion may be necessary if blood loss is significant (2B).
4.  **Identify and Treat Underlying Causes:**
    *   **Lacerations:** Repair any tears in the cervix, vagina, or perineum (2B).
    *   **Retained Tissue:** Manually remove any remaining placental fragments or membranes (2B).
    *   **Coagulopathy:** Correct any underlying bleeding disorders (2B).
5.  **Advanced Measures (if initial steps fail):**
    *   **Uterine Tamponade:** Balloon tamponade (e.g., Bakri balloon) can be used to apply pressure and control bleeding (2B).
    *   **Surgical Interventions:**
        *   Uterine compression sutures (e.g., B-Lynch suture) can help control atony (2B).
        *   Uterine artery embolization is an option for refractory bleeding (2B).
        *   Internal iliac artery balloon occlusion (IIABO) can reduce blood loss, particularly in cases like placenta accreta spectrum disorders (2B).
        *   Hysterectomy may be required as a last resort to control life-threatening hemorrhage (2B).

## Complications and prognosis

PPH can lead to severe complications, including:
*   Hypovolemic shock
*   Anemia
*   Acute kidney injury
*   Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)
*   Sheehan's syndrome (pituitary necrosis) (2B)
*   Hysterectomy
*   Death

With prompt and appropriate management, the prognosis for PPH is generally good. However, severe PPH remains a leading cause of maternal mortality globally (2B).

## Active research / open questions

Ongoing research focuses on:
*   Optimizing prophylactic strategies to prevent PPH.
*   Developing new uterotonic agents with improved efficacy and fewer side effects.
*   Improving the management of refractory PPH, including the role of minimally invasive techniques like uterine artery embolization and IIABO.
*   Understanding the long-term consequences of severe PPH, such as Sheehan's syndrome.
