## Clinical Definition / what it is

Placental abruption is the premature separation of the placenta from the uterine wall before delivery (2B). This separation can lead to bleeding, which may be visible (external) or concealed (internal), and can compromise fetal oxygenation and maternal hemodynamic stability.

## Epidemiology

The incidence of placental abruption is estimated to be around 1% of all pregnancies (2B). Risk factors include advanced maternal age, multiparity, prior abruption, smoking, cocaine use, hypertension, preeclampsia, trauma, and premature rupture of membranes (2B).

## Pathophysiology summary

The pathophysiology of placental abruption involves the disruption of the maternal-fetal interface, leading to bleeding into the decidua basalis and potentially into the uterine cavity. This separation can cause fetal distress due to reduced placental perfusion and oxygen delivery. Maternal complications can arise from hemorrhage and associated coagulopathy (2B).

## Evidence-based Management

Management depends on the severity of the abruption, gestational age, and maternal/fetal status.
*   **Mild abruption:** Expectant management may be considered in stable mothers with reassuring fetal status and preterm gestation (2B). This involves close monitoring of maternal vital signs, fetal heart rate, and uterine activity.
*   **Severe abruption:** Immediate delivery is typically indicated, often via cesarean section, especially if there is evidence of fetal distress or significant maternal hemorrhage (2B).
*   **Hemodynamic instability:** Management includes intravenous fluid resuscitation, blood transfusion if necessary, and correction of coagulopathy (2B).

## Complications and prognosis

Placental abruption is associated with significant maternal and fetal morbidity and mortality.
*   **Maternal complications:** Hemorrhage, shock, disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), renal failure, and hysterectomy (2B). Long-term maternal mortality risk is increased, particularly with placental retention, but abruption also carries an increased risk (HR 1.59, 95% CI 1.21-2.08, p < 0.001) (2B). However, this risk may be primarily driven by acute complications, as it lost significance after excluding deaths within 42 days (HR 1.31, p = 0.089) (2B).
*   **Fetal complications:** Fetal distress, hypoxia, preterm birth, intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR), and stillbirth (2B).

## Active research / open questions

Further research is needed to better understand the long-term maternal health outcomes following placental abruption, particularly the mechanisms driving the increased mortality risk observed in some studies (2B). The role of specific risk factors and the optimal management strategies for different presentations of abruption remain areas of investigation.

The cord inserts between the two placental lobes, either into a connecting chorionic bridge or into intervening membranes. Sonographic detection of a bilobate placenta that has a chorionic bridge—normal placental tissue connecting the two lobes—is not likely, particularly if not specifically sought. However, if placental lobes are separated by intervening membranes and the cord inserts directly into these membranes, such that the cord insertion is velamentous, then sonographic detection may help to avoid cord avul- sion at delivery. The umbilical cord inserts into the main body of the placenta, and prominent vessels may be visible cours- ing along the intervening membranes (Figs. 19-6 and 19-7).
