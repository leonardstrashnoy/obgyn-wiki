## Clinical Definition

Cervical incompetence (cervical insufficiency) is the painless dilatation of the cervix in the second trimester, typically resulting in recurrent pregnancy loss or preterm delivery before 24–28 weeks gestation (2B). It is a clinical diagnosis of exclusion made in the context of a characteristic obstetric history.

## Epidemiology

- Incidence: approximately 0.5–1% of all pregnancies (2B)
- Accounts for approximately 15% of deliveries between 16–28 weeks (2B)
- True incidence is difficult to determine due to lack of objective diagnostic criteria before cervical change occurs

## Risk Factors

### Prior Cervical Trauma (2B)
- Cervical conization (LEEP, cold knife, laser)
- Multiple dilation and curettage procedures
- Cervical laceration during prior delivery
- Mechanical dilation during pregnancy termination

### Congenital/Anatomic (2B)
- In utero diethylstilbestrol (DES) exposure
- Uterine anomalies (septate uterus, müllerian anomalies)
- Connective tissue disorders (Ehlers-Danlos)

### Other (2B)
- Multiple gestation
- Polyhydramnios (increased mechanical pressure)
- Prior cervical incompetence in previous pregnancy

## Pathophysiology

The cervix maintains pregnancy through a combination of fibrous connective tissue (primarily collagen) and smooth muscle (2B). Incompetence represents a structural failure of the internal os to remain closed against increasing uterine distension and pressure. Unlike preterm labor driven by infection/inflammation, cervical incompetence classically presents as:

- Painless cervical dilation
- Intact membranes bulging through the external os
- Minimal or absent uterine contractions

Histologically, affected cervices may show decreased collagen, increased smooth muscle, or disrupted architecture (2B).

## Diagnosis

### Historical Diagnosis (2B)
The diagnosis is primarily clinical based on obstetric history:
- One or more painless second-trimester losses
- Preterm deliveries with minimal labor symptoms
- Characteristic progression: bulging membranes → delivery

### Ultrasound Screening (2B)
- **Transvaginal cervical length (TVCL)** is the primary screening tool
- Cervical length < 25 mm before 24 weeks in high-risk patients is abnormal
- Cervical length < 20 mm in low-risk singletons associated with increased preterm birth risk
- **Funneling:** herniation of membranes into internal os; progressive if > 50% of cervical length
- Dynamic response to fundal pressure or Valsalva may unmask shortening

### Physical Examination
- Digital examination often reveals painless dilation with intact membranes
- Speculum exam confirms bulging membranes without bleeding or infection

## Evidence-based Management

### Cerclage (2B)

**Indications:**
- **History-indicated (prophylactic):** Based on prior cervical incompetence; placed at 12–14 weeks
- **Ultrasound-indicated:** TVCL < 25 mm before 24 weeks with prior preterm birth
- **Emergency/rescue:** Acute painless dilation with visible membranes in second trimester

**Technique:**
- McDonald cerclage: purse-string suture at cervicovaginal junction (most common)
- Shirodkar cerclage: dissection of bladder reflection, suture placed higher near internal os; preferred for very short cervix or prior failed McDonald
- Transabdominal cerclage: preconception or early pregnancy; used after failed vaginal cerclage or congenitally absent cervix

**Evidence:**
- History-indicated cerclage reduces recurrent second-trimester loss in high-risk women (2B)
- Ultrasound-indicated cerclage in singletons with prior preterm birth and TVCL < 25 mm reduces preterm delivery before 35 weeks (1B — Cervical Incompetence Prevention Randomized Cerclage Trial)
- Rescue cerclage may prolong pregnancy by 4–6 weeks; limited RCT data (2B)
- Cerclage is NOT beneficial in multiple gestations with short cervix (1B — detrimental in twins)

### Progesterone (1B)
- **Vaginal progesterone** (200 mg daily) for asymptomatic singletons with TVCL 10–20 mm before 24 weeks reduces preterm birth before 34 weeks
- Does not replace cerclage in women with prior cervical incompetence
- Not effective in multiple gestations

### Physical Measures (4)
- Activity restriction / bed rest: no evidence of benefit; may increase thromboembolic risk
- Pessary: controversial; some RCTs show benefit in selected populations, others negative
- Cervical occlusion (suturing external os): abandoned due to poor outcomes

### Emergency Cerclage Considerations (2B)
- Bed rest, Trendelenburg position, hydration
- Tocolysis (indomethacin, nifedipine) to reduce uterine irritability
- Prophylactic antibiotics
- Risks: membrane rupture, chorioamnionitis, bleeding, cervical laceration at delivery
- Contraindications: active bleeding, infection, ruptured membranes, regular contractions, fetal anomalies

## Complications

- Preterm premature rupture of membranes (PPROM)
- Chorioamnionitis
- Cervical laceration or tear at delivery requiring repair
- Suture displacement or erosion
- Failed cerclage with pregnancy loss

## Prognosis

With appropriate intervention (cerclage ± progesterone):
- Successful pregnancy rate > 85% in women with history-indicated cerclage (2B)
- Earlier placement (12–14 weeks) associated with better outcomes than emergency placement
- Prior failed cerclage → consider transabdominal cerclage (80–90% success)

## Differential Diagnosis

- Preterm labor (painful, with contractions)
- Chorioamnionitis (fever, uterine tenderness, leukocytosis)
- Placental abruption (painful bleeding)
- Second-trimester miscarriage from other etiologies (fetal anomalies, infection)

## Active Research / Open Questions

- Optimal cervical length thresholds for cerclage vs progesterone vs pessary
- Role of quantitative fetal fibronectin with short cervix
- Biomarkers (PAMG-1, IGFBP-1) for predicting cervical change
- Long-term outcomes after different cerclage techniques
- AI-assisted cervical length prediction from standard ultrasound
