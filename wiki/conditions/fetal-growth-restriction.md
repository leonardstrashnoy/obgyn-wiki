## Clinical Definition

Fetal Growth Restriction (FGR) is a pathological condition defined as estimated fetal weight (EFW) below the 10th percentile for gestational age, not explained by constitutional smallness (2B). It reflects failure of the fetus to achieve its genetically determined growth potential due to placental insufficiency or other pathological factors.

Distinction from **Small for Gestational Age (SGA)**: SGA is a statistical designation (EFW < 10th percentile) that may represent normal constitutional smallness, whereas FGR implies pathological restriction with increased perinatal risk (2B).

## Classification

- **Early-onset FGR:** Diagnosed before 32 weeks; associated with severe placental dysfunction and higher perinatal morbidity (2B)
- **Late-onset FGR:** Diagnosed after 32 weeks; more common, often milder, but still associated with adverse outcomes including stillbirth (2B)

## Etiology and Risk Factors

### Maternal Factors (2B)
- Chronic hypertension
- Preeclampsia (strong association)
- Diabetes mellitus (pregestational and gestational)
- Advanced maternal age
- Obesity / elevated BMI
- Smoking, alcohol, and substance use
- Poor nutritional status
- Autoimmune disease (e.g., antiphospholipid syndrome)

### Fetal/Placental Factors (2B)
- Chromosomal abnormalities and genetic syndromes
- Congenital infections (TORCH)
- Multiple pregnancy (twin-to-twin transfusion syndrome)
- Placental abruption
- Placental morphological abnormalities

## Pathophysiology

FGR is fundamentally a **placental disease** (2B):

1. **Inadequate trophoblast invasion** and **abnormal spiral artery remodeling** lead to reduced uteroplacental perfusion
2. **Placental hypoxia** triggers anti-angiogenic factor release (**sFlt-1** elevation, **sEng** elevation) similar to preeclampsia
3. **Endothelial dysfunction** at the maternal-fetal interface impairs nutrient and oxygen transfer
4. **Inflammatory activation** with elevated cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α) at the maternal-fetal interface contributes to placental injury

Molecular mechanisms (2B):
- **KISS1/KISS1R axis dysregulation**: altered expression affects trophoblast invasion
- **microRNA dysregulation**: placental miRNAs modulate angiogenesis and cell invasion
- **Impaired IGF signaling**: reduced insulin-like growth factor availability limits fetal growth

## Diagnosis

### Ultrasound Assessment (2B)
- **Estimated fetal weight (EFW)** < 10th percentile
- **Abdominal circumference (AC)** lagging most sensitive early indicator
- **Biophysical Profile (BPP)** scoring for fetal well-being

### Doppler Studies (2B)
- **Umbilical artery Doppler**: elevated resistance index / absent or reversed end-diastolic flow in severe cases
- **Middle cerebral artery (MCA) Doppler**: decreased pulsatility index (brain-sparing effect)
- **Uterine artery Doppler**: notching and elevated resistance in early screening

## Complications

- **Preterm birth**: often iatrogenic due to early delivery for fetal compromise (2B)
- **Stillbirth**: risk increases significantly with worsening Doppler abnormalities (2B)
- **Neonatal complications**: hypoglycemia, hypothermia, polycythemia, impaired immune function
- **Long-term consequences**: cardiovascular and metabolic programming, neurodevelopmental delay (2B)

## Management

### Surveillance (2B)
- Serial growth ultrasounds every 2–3 weeks
- Weekly or twice-weekly **Doppler monitoring** in severe cases
- **Non-stress tests (NST)** and **biophysical profile** for fetal well-being assessment
- Fetal kick counting

### Timing of Delivery (2B)
- Delivery when risk of intrauterine demise exceeds risk of neonatal morbidity
- Typically 37 weeks for late-onset FGR; earlier for severe early-onset with abnormal Doppler
- **Corticosteroids** for fetal lung maturation if delivery before 34 weeks anticipated

### Medical Interventions
- No proven pharmacologic therapy to reverse placental insufficiency
- **Low-dose aspirin** may have preventive role in high-risk nulliparous women (1B for prevention)
- Management of underlying maternal conditions (e.g., hypertension, diabetes)

## Related Conditions

- **Preeclampsia**: shared pathophysiology of abnormal trophoblast invasion; frequently co-occurs (2B)
- **Oligohydramnios**: often concurrent finding in severe placental insufficiency (2B)
- **Placental abruption**: shared vascular risk factors and placental pathology (2B)
- **Gestational diabetes**: can cause both macrosomia and, paradoxically, FGR in vascular complications (2B)

## Prognosis

With modern antenatal surveillance and timely delivery, perinatal mortality in FGR has decreased substantially. However, **early-onset severe FGR** with reversed umbilical artery flow remains among the highest-risk fetal conditions in obstetrics (2B).
