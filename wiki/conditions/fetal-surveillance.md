## Clinical Definition

Antepartum fetal surveillance encompasses the methods used to assess fetal well-being and placental function during pregnancy, particularly in high-risk pregnancies or when complications are suspected (2B). The goal is to identify fetal compromise before it results in stillbirth or severe morbidity, allowing for timely intervention.

## Epidemiology

- Fetal surveillance is employed in 10–15% of pregnancies considered high-risk (2B)
- Stillbirth rate in the United States: ~5.9 per 1000 births; antepartum surveillance aims to reduce preventable stillbirths (2B)
- In low-risk pregnancies, routine formal surveillance has not consistently reduced stillbirth rates (2B)

## Indications for Surveillance (2B)

### Maternal Indications
- Preeclampsia / chronic hypertension
- Diabetes mellitus (pre-gestational or gestational)
- Advanced maternal age (>35 years)
- Obesity (BMI ≥ 30)
- Autoimmune disease (SLE, antiphospholipid syndrome)
- Chronic renal disease
- Prior stillbirth

### Fetal/Placental Indications
- Fetal growth restriction (FGR / IUGR)
- Decreased fetal movement
- Oligohydramnios or polyhydramnios
- Multiple gestation
- Post-term pregnancy (>41 weeks)
- Isoimmunization

## Methods of Surveillance

### 1. Maternal Fetal Movement Assessment (2B)

**Technique:**
- Patients report perceived fetal movements ("kick counts")
- Various protocols: count to 10 movements within 2 hours, or time to 10 movements
- Encourage patients to report decreased movement from their perceived baseline

**Evidence:**
- One RCT (n=68,000 low-risk women) showed no difference in antepartum death between formal movement counting vs routine care (2.9 vs 2.7 per 1000); poor compliance (46%) limited interpretation (2B)
- Norwegian prospective cohort: written information + kick charts reduced stillbirth by ~50% in women presenting with decreased movement (OR 0.51, 95% CI 0.32–0.81) (2B)
- Cochrane review: insufficient evidence to recommend routine kick counting in low-risk pregnancies; no RCTs in high-risk pregnancies (2A)

### 2. Nonstress Test (NST) (2B)

**Technique:**
- External fetal heart rate (FHR) monitoring + tocodynamometer for 20–40 minutes
- Fetus is unstimulated (uterine quiescence expected)

**Interpretation:**
- **Reactive NST:** ≥ 2 FHR accelerations (≥ 15 bpm above baseline, ≥ 15 seconds duration) in 20 minutes
- **Nonreactive NST:** Fails to meet criteria in 40 minutes; may indicate fetal sleep cycle, CNS depressants, or fetal compromise

**Evidence:**
- Reactive NST correlates with absence of fetal metabolic acidemia in next 7 days (2B)
- False positive rate: 50–80%; nonreactive NST requires further testing (BPP or CST)
- Perinatal mortality within 1 week of reactive NST: 1.9 per 1000 (2B)

### 3. Biophysical Profile (BPP) (2B)

**Components (each scored 0 or 2, max 10 points):**
1. Fetal breathing movements (sustained >20 seconds)
2. Gross body movements (≥ 2 discrete movements in 30 min)
3. Fetal tone (extension with return to flexion)
4. Reactive NST
5. Amniotic fluid volume (AFI ≥ 5 cm or DVP ≥ 2 cm)

**Interpretation:**
- **8–10 / 10:** Normal; repeat weekly (twice weekly if diabetes / FGR / >41 weeks)
- **6 / 10:** Equivocal; deliver if oligohydramnios present; otherwise repeat in 24 hours
- **4 / 10:** Deliver (strong evidence of fetal compromise)
- **0–2 / 10:** Deliver urgently

**Evidence:**
- BPP score ≤ 4 associated with fetal acidemia and poor perinatal outcomes (2B)
- BPP has lower false positive rate than NST alone (2B)
- Modified BPP (NST + AFI only) commonly used for logistical efficiency in low-risk high-risk follow-up (2B)

### 4. Doppler Velocimetry (2B)

**Umbilical Artery (UA) Doppler:**
- Assesses placental resistance
- Normal: low impedance with forward end-diastolic flow
- **Absent end-diastolic flow (AEDF):** ~70% of placental arterial obliteration; high risk
- **Reversed end-diastolic flow (REDF):** Severe placental compromise; urgent delivery indicated

**Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) Doppler:**
- Brain-sparing reflex: reduced MCA impedance in fetal hypoxemia
- MCA-PI < 5th percentile or MCA/UA ratio < 1.08 suggests redistribution

**Uterine Artery Doppler:**
- Early screening tool (11–14 weeks or 20–24 weeks)
- Notching or high resistance predicts preeclampsia and FGR

**Evidence:**
- UA Doppler reduces perinatal mortality in high-risk / FGR pregnancies (1A — Cochrane)
- MCA Doppler helpful for timing delivery in early-onset FGR (2B)
- Ductus venosus Doppler (absent/reversed a-wave) predicts imminent fetal decompensation (2B)

## Evidence-based Management Algorithm

**Initial Assessment:**
1. Confirm indication for surveillance
2. Begin at 32–34 weeks (or earlier for severe conditions: 26–28 weeks)
3. Choose modality based on risk and resources

**Frequency (2B):**
| Condition | Test | Frequency |
|-----------|------|-----------|
| Diabetes (well-controlled) | NST or mBPP | Weekly from 32 weeks |
| Diabetes (poorly controlled / vascular) | NST or BPP | Twice weekly from 28–32 weeks |
| FGR | BPP + UA Doppler | Twice weekly |
| Preeclampsia (severe) | NST or BPP | Twice weekly; daily if inpatient |
| Post-term (>41 weeks) | NST or mBPP | Twice weekly |
| Decreased fetal movement | NST → BPP if nonreactive | Same day |

**Response to Abnormal Testing:**
- **Nonreactive NST:** Extend to 40 minutes; perform BPP; consider vibroacoustic stimulation
- **BPP ≤ 4:** Delivery indicated (mature fetus) or steroids + delivery planning (preterm)
- **REDF in UA:** Delivery unless extremely preterm; steroids if <34 weeks with monitoring
- **DV absent a-wave:** Delivery within 24–48 hours in most cases (2B)

## Complications and Limitations

- False positive tests cause unnecessary interventions (iatrogenic prematurity, cesarean delivery) (2B)
- False negatives occur (fetal death within 7 days of normal testing: ~0.8–1.9 per 1000) (2B)
- Cost and access limitations in low-resource settings
- Operator dependence for ultrasound components (AFI, BPP, Doppler)
- Maternal anxiety from testing protocols

## Active Research / Open Questions

- Optimal surveillance protocols for obese patients (poor NST signal, difficult ultrasound)
- Role of computer-aided FHR analysis vs visual interpretation
- Personalized surveillance timing based on biomarker panels (sFlt-1, PIGF)
- Home-based remote monitoring feasibility and efficacy
- Integration of AI with Doppler and BPP scoring
