# Ovarian Cancer

**Ovarian cancer** includes epithelial ovarian, fallopian tube, and primary peritoneal carcinoma. Most high-grade serous cancers are now understood to arise from the fallopian tube fimbria, but they present clinically as tubo-ovarian/peritoneal disease.

## Key Safety Point

There is no effective population screening test for average-risk asymptomatic patients. CA-125 and ultrasound can detect abnormalities but have insufficient positive predictive value for routine screening and can cause harm through false positives and unnecessary surgery.

## Risk Factors

- Age and postmenopausal status
- BRCA1/BRCA2 and homologous recombination repair mutations
- Lynch syndrome
- Strong family history of ovarian, breast, pancreatic, or prostate cancer
- Endometriosis (especially clear cell/endometrioid associations)
- Nulliparity and infertility

Protective factors include combined hormonal contraception, pregnancy, breastfeeding, and risk-reducing salpingo-oophorectomy for high-risk mutation carriers.

## Presentation

Symptoms are often nonspecific:

- Persistent bloating or increased abdominal size
- Early satiety or appetite change
- Pelvic or abdominal pain
- Urinary urgency/frequency
- Unintentional weight loss or fatigue
- Ascites or pleural effusion in advanced disease

New persistent symptoms, especially in postmenopausal patients, should prompt pelvic exam and imaging.

## Evaluation of Suspected Disease

### Initial Workup

1. Pelvic exam and assessment of ascites/nodularity.
2. Transvaginal and/or pelvic ultrasound to characterize adnexal mass.
3. CA-125 and additional markers as clinically appropriate.
4. Cross-sectional imaging when malignancy is suspected.
5. Referral to gynecologic oncology before surgery when risk is high.

### Features Suggesting Malignancy

- Solid components or papillary projections
- Thick septations
- Bilateral complex masses
- Ascites
- Peritoneal nodularity or omental caking
- Elevated CA-125 in postmenopausal patient
- Fixed/nodular pelvic mass

## Management

### Surgical Staging and Cytoreduction

Primary management generally includes staging/cytoreductive surgery by gynecologic oncology. The goal is complete gross resection when feasible. Procedures may include hysterectomy, bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy, omentectomy, peritoneal biopsies, lymph node assessment, and resection of visible disease.

### Primary Debulking vs Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy

Treatment sequence depends on resectability, patient fitness, disease distribution, and likelihood of optimal cytoreduction. Neoadjuvant platinum-taxane chemotherapy followed by interval debulking is appropriate when complete primary cytoreduction is unlikely or surgical morbidity is excessive.

### Systemic Therapy

Most epithelial ovarian cancers receive platinum-taxane chemotherapy. Maintenance therapy may include PARP inhibitors and/or anti-angiogenic therapy depending on BRCA/HRD status, stage, response, and prior regimen.

### Genetic Testing

All patients with epithelial ovarian cancer should be offered germline and somatic testing because results affect treatment, maintenance therapy, and cascade testing for relatives.

## Screening and Risk Reduction

Average-risk patients: do not screen with CA-125 or ultrasound.

High-risk mutation carriers: genetic counseling, individualized surveillance limitations discussion, and risk-reducing bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy after childbearing at gene-appropriate ages.
