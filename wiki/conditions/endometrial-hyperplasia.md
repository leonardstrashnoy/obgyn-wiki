# Endometrial Hyperplasia

**Endometrial hyperplasia** is an abnormal proliferation of the endometrial glands with an increase in the gland-to-stroma ratio. It represents a spectrum of lesions from benign proliferation to endometrial intraepithelial neoplasia (EIN) with malignant potential.

## Classification (WHO 2014 / Endometrial Intraepithelial Neoplasia System)

### Traditional WHO (4 categories)
1. **Simple hyperplasia without atypia** — 1% progression risk
2. **Complex hyperplasia without atypia** — 3% progression risk  
3. **Simple hyperplasia with atypia** — 8% progression risk
4. **Complex hyperplasia with atypia** — 29% progression risk

### EIN System (Preferred — dichotomous)
- **Hyperplasia** (benign, non-atypical): Low malignant potential
- **Endometrial Intraepithelial Neoplasia (EIN)**: Atypical hyperplasia; precancerous; >40% coexistent carcinoma at hysterectomy

*EIN system reduces interobserver variability and better predicts malignant risk.*

## Etiology and Risk Factors

### Unopposed Estrogen Stimulation
- **Anovulation**: PCOS, perimenopause, obesity
- **Exogenous estrogen**: Unopposed HRT
- **Estrogen-secreting tumors**: Granulosa cell tumors
- **Tamoxifen**: Weak estrogenic effect on endometrium

### Patient Risk Factors

| Factor | Association |
|--------|-------------|
| **Obesity** | Adipose aromatization of androgens → estrone |
| **Age** | Perimenopause (anovulation), postmenopause |
| **PCOS** | Chronic anovulation |
| **Diabetes** | Independent risk factor |
| **Lynch syndrome** | Hereditary risk |
| **Tamoxifen** | Weak estrogenic effect on endometrium |
| **Nulliparity** | Increased unopposed estrogen exposure |
| **Early menarche / late menopause** | Increased lifetime estrogen |

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms
- **Abnormal uterine bleeding** (90%): Heavy menstrual bleeding, intermenstrual bleeding, postmenopausal bleeding
- **Asymptomatic** (incidental finding on imaging or biopsy)

### Physical Exam
- May be normal
- Obesity often present
- Enlarged uterus (if coexisting fibroids)

## Diagnosis

### Endometrial Sampling

**Indications for biopsy**:
- Age ≥45 with AUB
- Postmenopausal bleeding
- Any age with risk factors + failed medical management

**Methods**:
- **Office endometrial biopsy** (Pipelle): 91% sensitive for hyperplasia
- **Hysteroscopic biopsy**: Gold standard; directed sampling (misses less)

### Imaging

**Transvaginal ultrasound**
- Endometrial thickness:
  - Premenopausal: Variable (5-14 mm in secretory phase is normal)
  - Postmenopausal: >4-5 mm warrants biopsy if bleeding
- **Heterogeneous appearance**, thickening
- Cannot reliably distinguish hyperplasia from early carcinoma

**Sonohysterography**
- Better delineates focal vs diffuse lesions
- Distinguishes polyps from hyperplasia

**MRI**
- Not first-line
- May help assess myometrial invasion if carcinoma suspected

### Histopathology

**Findings**:
- **Non-atypical**: Gland-to-stroma ratio >1:1, crowded glands, ± cystic dilation
- **Atypical (EIN)**: Cytologic atypia (enlarged nuclei, prominent nucleoli), architectural complexity, cribriform growth

**Immunohistochemistry**: PTEN loss in EIN pathway

## Management

### Non-Atypical Hyperplasia

**First-line: Progestin therapy** (Level: 2A)

| Regimen | Dose | Duration |
|---------|------|----------|
| **Medroxyprogesterone** | 10-20 mg daily | 3-6 months |
| **Micronized progesterone** | 200-300 mg daily | 3-6 months |
| **LNG-IUD** | 52 mg | First-line preferred |

- **Response rate**: 80-90%
- **Follow-up**: Repeat biopsy after 3-6 months
- **Hysterectomy**: Consider if treatment fails ×2, completed childbearing, or patient preference

### Endometrial Intraepithelial Neoplasia (Atypical)

**Hysterectomy** — Standard of care (Level: 2A)
- Recommended for most patients (especially if completed childbearing)
- Risk of coexistent cancer: 40-50%

**Fertility-sparing** — For select patients (Level: 2B)
- Requirements:
  - Strong desire for fertility
  - No contraindications to pregnancy
  - Understanding of cancer risk
- **Regimen**: High-dose progestin
  - Medroxyprogesterone 400-600 mg daily
  - Or megestrol 160-320 mg daily
  - Or LNG-IUD (off-label but effective)
- **Follow-up**: Sampler biopsy every 3-6 months
- **Duration**: Minimum 6-12 months; attempt conception once complete response
- **Risk**: Delayed cancer diagnosis if surveillance inadequate

## Prevention

### Risk Reduction Strategies
| Strategy | Application |
|----------|-------------|
| **Weight loss** | Obese patients with PCOS |
| **Cyclic progestins** | Chronic anovulation (PCOS), perimenopause |
| **LNG-IUD** | High-risk patients needing contraception |
| **Combined HRT** | Postmenopausal (progestin essential if uterus present) |
| **Tamoxifen alternatives** | Aromatase inhibitors for breast cancer |

## Screening

**Not recommended** for average-risk women.

**High-risk** (Lynch syndrome, tamoxifen, chronic anovulation):
- Annual endometrial sampling beginning age 35
- Transvaginal ultrasound with clinical discretion
