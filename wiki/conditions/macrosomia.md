## Clinical Definition

Fetal macrosomia is typically defined as estimated fetal weight ≥ 4000 g (some use ≥ 4500 g). It is associated with increased risks of shoulder dystocia, cesarean delivery, birth trauma, and neonatal complications (2B).

## Risk Factors

- Maternal diabetes (pregestational and gestational)
- Prior macrosomic infant
- Maternal obesity / elevated BMI
- Excessive gestational weight gain
- Postterm pregnancy
- Multiparity
- Male sex

## Complications

- Shoulder dystocia
- Brachial plexus injury
- Clavicle fracture
- Cesarean delivery
- Postpartum hemorrhage (uterine atony)
- Neonatal hypoglycemia (especially if maternal diabetes)
- Birth asphyxia

## Management

- **Diabetes control:** Tight glycemic management reduces macrosomia risk
- **Ultrasound estimation:** EFW > 4000 g at term; accuracy ± 10–15%
- **Delivery planning:** Consider elective cesarean if EFW > 5000 g (non-diabetic) or > 4500 g (diabetic) — weak evidence, individualized
- **Labor management:** Avoid prolonged second stage; prepare for shoulder dystocia

## Evidence Summary

| Claim | Evidence Level | Basis |
|-------|-------------|-------|
| Maternal diabetes strongest modifiable risk | 2B | Cohort data |
| Ultrasound EFW inaccurate by ±10–15% | 2B | Validation studies |
| Prophylactic cesarean for macrosomia debated | 2B | ACOG + limited RCTs |
| Tight glycemic control reduces risk | 1B | RCT data |
