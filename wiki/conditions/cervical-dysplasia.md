# Cervical Dysplasia

**Cervical dysplasia** is premalignant squamous or glandular epithelial change of the cervix, usually driven by persistent high-risk human papillomavirus (HPV). Modern terminology uses squamous intraepithelial lesion (SIL) cytology and cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN) histology.

## Classification

| Domain | Low Grade | High Grade |
|---|---|---|
| Cytology | LSIL | HSIL, ASC-H |
| Histology | CIN 1 | CIN 2, CIN 3 |
| Biology | Productive HPV infection | Transforming persistent HPV infection |

Adenocarcinoma in situ (AIS) is a glandular precursor lesion and requires separate management from squamous CIN.

## Risk Factors

- Persistent high-risk HPV, especially HPV 16 and 18
- Early sexual debut and multiple partners
- Smoking
- Immunosuppression, including HIV or transplant immunosuppression
- Inadequate screening history
- Lack of HPV vaccination

## Natural History

Most HPV infections clear spontaneously, especially in younger patients. CIN 1 frequently regresses and is usually observed. CIN 3 is the most reproducible high-grade precursor and carries substantial progression risk if untreated. CIN 2 has intermediate biology and may regress, especially in adolescents and young adults.

## Diagnosis

### Screening Trigger

Abnormal cytology or positive high-risk HPV testing routes to risk-based management. The result should be interpreted with age, current test combination, prior screening history, HPV genotype, and immune status.

### Colposcopy

Colposcopy evaluates the transformation zone using acetic acid and directed biopsy. Endocervical sampling is considered when the squamocolumnar junction is not fully visualized, cytology is high-grade, glandular abnormality is present, or no lesion is seen despite significant risk.

### Histology

Management is based on histologic diagnosis and estimated immediate CIN3+ risk, not cytology alone.

## Management

### CIN 1

Observation is preferred because regression is common. Treatment may be considered if CIN 1 persists for at least 2 years or follow-up reliability is poor.

### CIN 2

Management depends on age, fertility goals, transformation-zone visualization, and patient preference. Treatment is generally recommended for most adults, but observation can be acceptable in selected patients, especially younger patients desiring fertility preservation and able to comply with surveillance.

### CIN 3

Treatment is recommended. Excisional therapy is preferred when invasion cannot be excluded, cytology is high-grade, glandular disease is suspected, or the lesion extends into the canal.

### Treatment Options

| Treatment | Role | Key Tradeoff |
|---|---|---|
| LEEP / LLETZ | Common outpatient excisional treatment | Provides specimen; may increase preterm birth risk with deeper excision |
| Cold-knife cone | Suspected glandular disease or invasion | Better margins; higher procedural morbidity |
| Ablation | Selected visible ectocervical CIN without invasion concern | No specimen; strict eligibility required |
| Hysterectomy | Not primary treatment for CIN alone | Reserved for selected recurrent disease or concurrent uterine indication |

## Follow-up

Post-treatment surveillance uses HPV-based testing because recurrence risk persists for years. A negative HPV-based test is more reassuring than cytology alone.

## Management Algorithm

```text
Abnormal cervical screen
        |
        v
Estimate CIN3+ risk using current result + history + HPV genotype
        |
        +--> low immediate risk -> repeat HPV-based testing at guideline interval
        |
        +--> colposcopy threshold met -> colposcopy + biopsy +/- endocervical sampling
        |
        v
Histology
        |
        +--> CIN 1 -> observation preferred
        +--> CIN 2 -> treatment vs selected observation
        +--> CIN 3/AIS/suspected invasion -> excisional treatment / oncology pathway
```
