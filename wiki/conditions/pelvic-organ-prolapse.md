# Pelvic Organ Prolapse

**Pelvic organ prolapse (POP)** is descent of the anterior vaginal wall, posterior vaginal wall, uterus/cervix, or vaginal apex due to pelvic floor support failure. Treatment is guided by symptoms and bother, not anatomic stage alone.

## Compartments

| Compartment | Common Terms | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| Anterior | Cystocele | Vaginal bulge, urinary symptoms |
| Apical | Uterine prolapse, vault prolapse | Bulge, pressure, incomplete emptying |
| Posterior | Rectocele, enterocele | Splinting, obstructed defecation |

## Risk Factors

- Vaginal childbirth, especially operative birth or obstetric anal sphincter injury
- Age and menopause
- Connective tissue vulnerability
- Chronic increased intra-abdominal pressure: constipation, chronic cough, heavy lifting
- Obesity
- Prior hysterectomy or pelvic surgery

## Evaluation

- Symptom assessment: bulge, pressure, splinting, voiding dysfunction, bowel symptoms, sexual function.
- Pelvic exam with Valsalva, ideally standing if symptoms exceed supine findings.
- POP-Q or simplified staging to document compartment and severity.
- Assess urinary incontinence; reduction of prolapse may unmask stress leakage.
- Evaluate ulceration, bleeding, infection, or urinary retention when present.

## Management

### Observation

Asymptomatic or minimally bothersome prolapse can be observed. Lifestyle measures include constipation management, weight optimization, and avoiding chronic straining when possible.

### Pelvic Floor Muscle Training

Pelvic floor muscle training can improve symptoms and prevent progression in mild to moderate prolapse. It is often combined with conservative UI management.

### Pessary

Pessaries provide non-surgical support and are appropriate for many symptomatic patients, including those desiring fertility preservation, avoiding surgery, awaiting surgery, or with high surgical risk.

Key points:

- Requires fitting and follow-up.
- Vaginal estrogen may reduce irritation in postmenopausal atrophy.
- Complications: discharge, erosions, bleeding, expulsion; serious complications are rare with follow-up.

### Surgery

Surgery is for bothersome prolapse when conservative therapy fails or is declined.

Options include:

- Native tissue anterior/posterior repair
- Apical suspension (uterosacral or sacrospinous ligament fixation)
- Sacrocolpopexy for selected apical prolapse
- Obliterative surgery for patients not desiring vaginal intercourse
- Hysterectomy or uterine-sparing repair depending on anatomy, preference, cancer risk, and surgeon expertise

Apical support is crucial; isolated anterior/posterior repair without apical correction increases recurrence risk when apical descent is present.
