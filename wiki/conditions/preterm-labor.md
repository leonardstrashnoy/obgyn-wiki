## Clinical Definition / what it is

Preterm labor is defined as regular uterine contractions accompanied by cervical change (dilation and/or effacement) occurring before 37 completed weeks of gestation (1A). It is a leading cause of preterm birth, which is birth before 37 weeks of gestation (2B).

## Epidemiology (incidence, risk factors if known)

Preterm birth is a significant global health issue. Women with pre-existing mental health problems have higher odds and risks of adverse birth outcomes, including preterm birth (2B). Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) and hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (HDP) are also associated with adverse pregnancy outcomes, including preterm birth (2B). The joint effect of GDM and HDP may increase the risk of adverse outcomes (2B).

## Pathophysiology summary

The exact pathophysiology of preterm labor is complex and multifactorial, often involving inflammation, infection, uterine stretch, and hormonal changes. While specific mechanisms are not detailed in the provided sources, conditions like Sheehan syndrome (resulting from postpartum hemorrhage) can lead to hormonal imbalances (hypopituitarism) that might indirectly affect pregnancy outcomes, though this is a rare presentation (2B).

## Evidence-based Management (with evidence levels cited inline)

Management strategies aim to prolong pregnancy and improve neonatal outcomes. Specific interventions are not detailed in the provided sources, but general principles include monitoring, potential tocolysis (medications to stop contractions), administration of corticosteroids for fetal lung maturation, and magnesium sulfate for neuroprotection in anticipated preterm birth <32 weeks (1A). Management of underlying conditions like GDM and HDP is crucial (2B).

## Complications and prognosis

Preterm birth is associated with significant neonatal morbidity and mortality. Complications can include respiratory distress syndrome, intraventricular hemorrhage, necrotizing enterocolitis, and long-term neurodevelopmental issues (2B). The prognosis depends heavily on the gestational age at birth and the severity of complications.

## Active research / open questions

Research continues to explore the complex interplay of factors contributing to preterm labor and birth. Understanding the precise mechanisms, identifying better predictors, and developing more effective preventative and therapeutic strategies remain key areas of investigation. The interaction between conditions like GDM and HDP and their impact on preterm birth requires further study (2B).

Translabial Ultrasound Translabial (also known as transperineal) ultrasound (TLU) was first described in France in the early 1980s. This technique involves having the woman lie on the examination table with her hips and knees flexed. Adjusting the examination table to place her in a Trendelenburg posi­ tion can be helpful. A covered transducer (using a probe cover, or glove, and sterile gel) is positioned in a sagittal orientation on the perineum between the labia majora at the level of the vaginal introitus (Fig. 18-1). Elevation of the woman’s hips with a cushion or by positioning her hands to achieve a pelvic tilt can be used to improve visualization. Unlike TAU, this technique does not require bladder filling, is not impaired by obscuration by fetal parts, and with the transducer closer to the cervix, achieves close to 100% visualization.
