# Bacterial Vaginosis

**Bacterial vaginosis (BV)** is a dysbiosis of vaginal flora characterized by reduced lactobacilli and overgrowth of anaerobic organisms. It commonly presents with thin discharge and fishy odor, but many patients are asymptomatic.

## Clinical Features

- Thin white or gray discharge
- Fishy odor, often after intercourse or menses
- Vaginal pH >4.5
- Minimal vulvar inflammation compared with candidiasis
- Microscopy may show clue cells and reduced lactobacilli

## Diagnosis

### Amsel Criteria

BV is supported by at least 3 of 4:

1. Homogeneous thin discharge
2. Vaginal pH >4.5
3. Positive whiff test with KOH
4. Clue cells on microscopy

### Nugent Score / NAAT

Gram stain Nugent scoring is a reference laboratory method. NAAT panels can improve diagnostic accuracy and are useful when microscopy is unavailable or symptoms recur.

## Management

Treat symptomatic BV. Common first-line regimens include metronidazole or clindamycin by oral or vaginal route depending on tolerance, pregnancy status, preference, and contraindications.

Counseling:

- Avoid alcohol counseling may be given with metronidazole depending on local guidance and patient tolerance.
- Avoid douching; it increases recurrence risk.
- Routine treatment of male partners is not recommended for typical BV.
- Test for STIs when risk factors or cervicitis symptoms are present.

## Pregnancy

Symptomatic BV in pregnancy should be treated. BV is associated with adverse pregnancy outcomes, but routine screening/treatment of asymptomatic low-risk pregnant patients has not consistently improved outcomes.

## Recurrent BV

Recurrence is common. Strategies may include suppressive metronidazole gel, induction therapy followed by suppression, and evaluation for alternative diagnoses. Probiotics have mixed evidence and are not a substitute for evidence-based antimicrobial regimens.
