# Endometrial Cancer

**Endometrial cancer** is malignancy arising from the uterine lining. Most cases present with abnormal uterine bleeding, especially postmenopausal bleeding, and are diagnosed by endometrial sampling.

## Classification

Modern classification integrates histology and molecular subtype.

| Group | Typical Pattern | Clinical Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Endometrioid, low grade | Estrogen-related, often preceded by hyperplasia/EIN | Common; usually favorable if early stage |
| Serous / clear cell / carcinosarcoma | Non-endometrioid high-risk histology | Higher recurrence risk; aggressive management |
| POLE-mutated | Ultramutated | Excellent prognosis in many cohorts |
| MMR-deficient | Lynch-associated or sporadic MLH1 methylation | Needs Lynch evaluation pathway |
| p53-abnormal | Serous-like biology | Higher-risk adjuvant decisions |

## Risk Factors

- Unopposed estrogen exposure
- Obesity
- Chronic anovulation / PCOS
- Nulliparity and infertility
- Tamoxifen exposure
- Lynch syndrome
- Age and postmenopausal status
- Diabetes and metabolic syndrome

Protective factors include combined hormonal contraception, progestin exposure, and parity.

## Presentation

- Postmenopausal bleeding is the classic presentation.
- Premenopausal patients may present with heavy, prolonged, or irregular bleeding.
- Advanced disease can cause pelvic pain, weight loss, or abnormal discharge.

## Diagnosis

### Initial Evaluation

1. Exclude pregnancy when relevant.
2. Assess bleeding pattern, risk factors, anemia, and medications.
3. Perform pelvic exam to localize bleeding and evaluate cervical/vaginal lesions.

### Endometrial Sampling

Endometrial biopsy is indicated for:

- Postmenopausal bleeding with concerning ultrasound or persistent symptoms
- Age ≥45 with abnormal uterine bleeding
- Age <45 with persistent AUB plus risk factors such as obesity, chronic anovulation, failed medical therapy, or Lynch risk
- Known EIN/endometrial hyperplasia surveillance

### Ultrasound

Transvaginal ultrasound can triage postmenopausal bleeding when the endometrial stripe is thin and symptoms are not persistent. In premenopausal patients, endometrial thickness thresholds are less reliable; clinical risk drives sampling.

## Management

### Early-Stage Surgical Management

Standard management is total hysterectomy with bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy, surgical staging, and nodal assessment based on risk. Minimally invasive surgery is preferred when feasible.

### Fertility-Sparing Management

Selected patients with grade 1 endometrioid carcinoma or EIN confined to the endometrium may be candidates for high-dose progestin or LNG-IUD therapy with close sampling surveillance. This requires specialist counseling because progression or occult invasion can occur.

### Adjuvant Therapy

Adjuvant radiation, chemotherapy, or combined therapy depends on stage, grade, histology, lymphovascular invasion, depth of myometrial invasion, nodal status, and molecular subtype.

### Survivorship

Follow-up focuses on symptoms, pelvic exam when indicated, management of treatment effects, weight/metabolic risk reduction, and prompt evaluation of vaginal bleeding or new pelvic symptoms.
