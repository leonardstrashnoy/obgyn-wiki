# Leiomyomas (Uterine Fibroids)

**Uterine leiomyomas** (fibroids) are benign smooth muscle tumors of the uterus and represent the most common pelvic tumors in women. They are a leading structural cause of abnormal uterine bleeding and reproductive dysfunction.

## Epidemiology

- Prevalence: 20-80% of women by age 50 (varies by population and detection method)
- Higher incidence in African American women
- Hormonally responsive: grow during reproductive years, regress postmenopause

## Pathophysiology

Monoclonal proliferation of smooth muscle cells surrounded by compressed pseudocapsule.
- **Estrogen** and **progesterone** stimulate growth
- Local growth factors (IGF-1, TGF-β) mediate proliferation
- Genetic factors: MED12 mutations in ~70% of fibroids

## Classification

By location (FIGO classification):
- **Type 0**: Pedunculated intracavitary
- **Type 1-2**: Submucosal (distort endometrial cavity)
- **Type 3**: Intramural
- **Type 4**: Subserosal
- **Type 5-7**: Subserosal with intramural component
- **Type 8**: Other (cervical, parasitic)

### Clinical Significance

Submucosal (Types 0-2) most likely to cause:
- Heavy menstrual bleeding
- Reproductive dysfunction (infertility, pregnancy loss)

Intramural/subserosal may cause:
- Bulk symptoms (pelvic pressure, urinary frequency, constipation)
- Incidental finding

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms
- Heavy menstrual bleeding (defined as >80mL blood loss per cycle)
- Prolonged menses (>7 days)
- Pelvic pressure/pain
- Reproductive dysfunction
- Urinary symptoms (frequency, obstruction from anterior fibroid)
- Constipation (posterior fibroid)

### Physical Examination
- Enlarged, irregularly contoured uterus on bimanual exam
- Size typically described as weeks of gestation

## Diagnosis

### First-line Imaging
**Transvaginal ultrasound** (Evidence: 1A)
- Hypoechoic, well-circumscribed masses with posterior shadowing
- Saline-infusion sonohysterography for submucosal characterization

### Advanced Imaging
- **MRI**: Gold standard for preoperative mapping; distinguishes from adenomyosis
- **Hysteroscopy**: Direct visualization of submucosal fibroids

## Management

### Expectant Management
Indicated for:
- Asymptomatic fibroids <3-4 cm
- Postmenopausal patients
- Near menopause with minimal symptoms

### Medical Therapy

**Hormonal contraception** (Level: 2B)
- Combined oral contraceptives: Reduce bleeding 20-50%
- Progestin-only: LNG-IUD reduces bleeding up to 90% at 12 months

**GnRH agonists** (Level: 2A)
- Temporary shrinkage (30-50%) and amenorrhea
- Preoperative use or bridge to menopause
- Limit: 6 months (bone loss risk)

**Ulipristal acetate** (Level: 2B)
- Selective progesterone receptor modulator
- Preoperative fibroid reduction
- Availability varies by region

### Surgical Management

**Myomectomy** — Fertility preservation (Level: 2A)
- Indicated for: Symptomatic fibroids with reproductive goals
- Approach: Hysteroscopic, laparoscopic, or open
- Risk: Recurrence 10-30% over 5 years

**Hysterectomy** — Definitive therapy (Level: 1A)
- Indicated for: Completed childbearing, failed conservative management
- Most common definitive treatment for symptomatic fibroids

**Uterine artery embolization (UAE)** (Level: 2A)
- Radiologic intervention occluding uterine arteries
- 85-90% symptom improvement at 1 year
- Risk: Ovarian failure (1-5%), post-embolization syndrome

### Emerging Therapies
- **Focused ultrasound surgery (MRgFUS)**: Non-invasive thermal ablation
- **Laparoscopic radiofrequency ablation**: Minimally invasive destruction

## Special Considerations

### Pregnancy
- Risk: Pain (degeneration), malpresentation, postpartum hemorrhage
- Most fibroids do not complicate pregnancy significantly
- C-section if large lower uterine segment fibroid obstructs birth canal

### Red Flags for Malignancy (Leiomyosarcoma)
- Rapid growth in postmenopausal woman
- Single, rapidly enlarging dominant mass
- Suspicious imaging features (hemorrhage, necrosis)
- Note: Incidence <0.1% of presumed fibroids; morcellation contraindicated if suspected
