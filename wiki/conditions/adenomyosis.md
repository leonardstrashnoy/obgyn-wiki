# Adenomyosis

**Adenomyosis** is a benign gynecologic condition characterized by the presence of endometrial glands and stroma within the myometrium, causing reactive hypertrophy and hyperplasia of surrounding smooth muscle.

## Pathophysiology

- Ectopic endometrial tissue penetrates into myometrium (junctional zone disruption)
- Induces hypertrophy and hyperplasia of adjacent smooth muscle
- Creates a diffusely enlarged, globular uterus with poor contractility
- Theories: Tissue trauma/invasion, metaplasia, developmental origin

## Epidemiology

- Prevalence: 20-35% of women (varies by diagnostic method)
- Age distribution: 40-50 years (parous women)
- Increasing recognition in infertile patients and younger women with improved imaging

## Clinical Presentation

### Classic Triad
1. **Dysmenorrhea** — progressively worsening (50-80%)
2. **Heavy menstrual bleeding** (menorrhagia) — 40-50%
3. **Pelvic pain** — chronic or cyclic

### Additional Features
- Dyspareunia (20%)
- Tenderness on exam (especially at uterine fundus)
- Diffuse uterine enlargement (typically symmetric, <12 weeks size)

### Reproductive Impact
- Associated with infertility
- Increased risk of pregnancy complications (preterm birth, preeclampsia)
- Mechanism: Altered uterine peristalsis, inflammation, implantation defects

## Diagnosis

### Clinical
- Symptoms + tender, bulky (symmetric) uterus on bimanual exam
- Often underdiagnosed clinically

### Imaging

**Transvaginal ultrasound (TVUS)** (Evidence: 2A)
- **Findings**:
  - Asymmetric thickening of myometrial wall
  - Heterogeneous myometrial echotexture
  - Myometrial cysts (1-5 mm anechoic spaces)
  - Linear striations radiating from endometrium
  - Ill-defined endometrial-myometrial junction
- **Sensitivity**: 65-80% for experienced operators

**MRI** — Gold standard (Evidence: 1B)
- **Diagnostic features**:
  - Thickened junctional zone (>12 mm)
  - High-signal T2 foci (endometrial islands)
  - Low-signal T2 linear striations
- **Sensitivity**: 85-90%
- Indicated when TVUS equivocal or for surgical planning

### Histological
- **Definitive diagnosis**: Hysterectomy specimen
- Incidental finding in >40% of hysterectomies

## Differential Diagnosis

| Feature | Adenomyosis | Leiomyomas |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| Uterus | Symmetric, globular, tender | Asymmetric, discrete nodules, firm |
| Menorrhagia | Common | Common |
| Dysmenorrhea | Severe, progressive | Variable |
| Imaging | Diffuse, poorly marginated | Well-circumscribed masses |

See [[leiomyomas]] for comparison.

## Management

### Medical Therapy

**Hormonal contraception** (Level: 2B)
- Combined oral contraceptives: Reduce bleeding and pain
- LNG-IUD: Effective for menorrhagia (50-90% reduction)
- Continuous progestins: Suppress cyclicity, reduce symptoms

**GnRH agonists** (Level: 2B)
- Temporary symptom control and uterine shrinkage
- Bridge to menopause or surgery
- Limit to 6 months (bone loss)

**NSAIDs** (Level: 2B)
- Adjunctive for dysmenorrhea
- Effect: Moderate

**Aromatase inhibitors** (Level: 3)
- Experimental; reduces estrogen production
- Limited data

### Surgical Management

**Hysterectomy** — Definitive therapy (Level: 1A)
- Indicated for: Completed childbearing, refractory symptoms
- Most effective long-term treatment

**Conservative surgery**
- **Adenomyoma excision**: Focal disease in reproductive-aged patients
- **Endometrial ablation**: For menorrhagia without deep myometrial involvement
- **Uterine artery embolization**: Mixed results, fertility unknown

### Fertility Preservation

Challenges:
- Medical therapy often incompatible with conception
- Surgery (focal excision) technically difficult, recurrence common
- Assisted reproductive technology: Variable success rates

**Approach**:
- Mild disease: Expectant management + IVF if needed
- Severe symptoms: Shared decision-making regarding hysterectomy
