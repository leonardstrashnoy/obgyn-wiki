## Clinical Definition

Uterine atony is the failure of the myometrium to contract effectively after delivery of the placenta, resulting in inability to compress placental bed vessels and leading to postpartum hemorrhage. It is the leading cause of postpartum hemorrhage worldwide (1A).

## Epidemiology

- Accounts for 70–80% of postpartum hemorrhage cases (1A)
- Incidence of PPH: 5–10% of all deliveries; atony is the dominant etiology
- Higher risk after prolonged labor, multiple gestation, polyhydramnios, macrosomia, chorioamnionitis

## Risk Factors

- Uterine overdistension (multiple pregnancy, polyhydramnios, macrosomia)
- Prolonged or precipitous labor
- Chorioamnionitis
- Uterine relaxants (magnesium sulfate, halogenated anesthetics)
- High parity
- Placental abruption
- Full bladder (obstructing uterine contraction)

## Pathophysiology

Normal postpartum hemostasis depends on myometrial contraction compressing spiral arteries in the placental bed. When myometrial tone is inadequate:
- Spiral arteries remain patent
- Blood loss continues until contraction occurs or surgical intervention
- Coagulopathy may develop secondary to massive hemorrhage

## Evidence-based Management

**First-line (4Ts approach to PPH):**
1. **Tone (atony):** Uterine massage + oxytocin 10 IU IM or 5–10 IU IV
2. **Tissue:** Inspect for retained placenta / clots
3. **Trauma:** Examine for lacerations
4. **Thrombin:** Assess for coagulopathy

**Escalation if oxytocin fails:**
- Methylergonovine 0.2 mg IM (contraindicated in hypertension)
- Carboprost 250 mcg IM every 15 min (max 2 mg)
- Misoprostol 800–1000 mcg PR/SL
- Tranexamic acid 1 g IV (WOMAN trial) (1A)

**Surgical if medical fails:**
- Uterine balloon tamponade (Bakri balloon)
- Uterine artery embolization
- B-Lynch suture / compression sutures
- Hysterectomy (life-saving last resort)
