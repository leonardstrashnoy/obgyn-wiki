## Clinical Definition

"Fetal distress" is a deprecated umbrella term for signs suggesting fetal compromise requiring urgent evaluation. Modern terminology favors **"nonreassuring fetal status" (NRFS)** or specific descriptors (fetal heart rate category III, biophysical profile < 4/10, absent umbilical artery end-diastolic flow) (2B).

## Diagnostic Criteria

**Fetal Heart Rate (ACOG categories):**
- **Category I (normal):** Baseline 110–160, moderate variability, no late/variable decelerations, early decelerations acceptable
- **Category II (indeterminate):** Everything not I or III
- **Category III (abnormal):** Absent variability + recurrent late decelerations, or absent variability + bradycardia, or sinusoidal pattern → **urgent intervention** (2B)

**Other markers:**
- BPP ≤ 4/10
- Persistent late decelerations
- Prolonged deceleration > 2 minutes
- Umbilical artery REDF

## Management

- **Intrauterine resuscitation:** Left lateral position, oxygen, IV fluids, stop oxytocin, tocolysis if tachysystole, correct hypotension
- **Delivery:** If category III persists despite resuscitation → urgent delivery (cesarean or operative vaginal if fully dilated) (2B)
- **Scalp stimulation:** FHR acceleration after scalp stimulation suggests absence of acidemia

## Controversies

- The term "fetal distress" has poor predictive value; overuse led to many unnecessary cesarean deliveries
- Category II comprises ~80% of tracings; management is highly variable and operator-dependent
- Fetal scalp pH sampling largely abandoned; replaced by fetal scalp stimulation and improved FHR interpretation
